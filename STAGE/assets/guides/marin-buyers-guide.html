The Marin Buyer's Guide

A clear-eyed walkthrough of buying a home in Marin County — from first call to final closing, with a few honest words on what makes this market its own.

Before you start looking

The most common mistake first-time Marin buyers make is starting the property hunt before they've done the inside work. Three things to settle first:

  1. Your real budget. Pre-approval from a lender, with paperwork in hand. Online estimators are a starting point, not a number an offer can hang on.
  2. Your non-negotiables. Bedrooms, school zone, commute, accessibility — the three or four things that, if missing, mean the home is wrong. Everything else is a nice-to-have.
  3. Your timeline. Marin moves at its own pace. Some buyers are ready in 90 days; others spend a year. Both are fine. What matters is being honest about which one you are.
"I'd rather work with a buyer for nine months and find them the right home than rush them into the wrong one in six weeks."

Understanding the Marin micro-markets

Marin isn't one market. It's a constellation of neighborhoods, each with its own rhythm. A few quick generalizations to start a conversation — never to end one:

Southern Marin (Mill Valley, Tiburon, Sausalito, Belvedere)

Closest to the city, oldest housing stock, steepest prices. Inventory turns slowly and homes often go off-market through networks before they ever hit the MLS. Working with a local agent matters most here.

Central Marin (Larkspur, Corte Madera, San Anselmo, Ross, Kentfield)

Family-favorite school districts, mix of cottages and substantial homes. More inventory than the south, still tight. Walking-town life in San Anselmo and Larkspur is part of the appeal.

Northern Marin (San Rafael, Novato)

Most varied price range and largest inventory. Genuine starter-home opportunities still exist, especially in Terra Linda and parts of Novato. Newer construction is more common here.

West Marin (Pt Reyes, Bolinas, Inverness, Stinson Beach)

A separate world. Limited inventory, second-home and weekend-property territory, weather-dependent commute. Worth the conversation only if you know why you want to be there.

The buying process, step by step

1Get pre-approved

Not pre-qualified — pre-approved, with real documents. Marin sellers and listing agents take pre-approval seriously, and the strongest offers are the ones that look bulletproof on financing.

2Define your search

We'll talk through neighborhoods, must-haves, and budget. I'll set up custom searches that surface new listings the moment they hit the MLS — and quietly flag pre-market opportunities through agent channels.

3Tour homes — thoughtfully

Open houses are useful but they're crowded. Private showings let us slow down, look in cabinets, sit in rooms, take time. I prefer to limit each session to three or four homes — more than that and they all blur together.

4Run the disclosures before you fall in love

Most Marin listings provide a disclosure packet up front: inspections, pest reports, geological surveys, permit history. Read these before writing an offer. A short call with the inspector who wrote a report often answers more than a re-read of the document.

5Write a strong offer

Multi-offer situations are common. A strong offer isn't always the highest one — it's the cleanest one. Few contingencies, large earnest money, demonstrated financing, and a thoughtful personal letter (where appropriate) often beat raw dollars.

6Inspect, negotiate, close

Inspection contingencies are valuable for major surprises — foundation, roof, structural — not for nitpicking. Plan to handle the small stuff yourself. Typical Marin escrow runs 21 to 30 days.

A few honest truths

  • The "perfect" home doesn't exist. The right home solves your most important problems and lets you live with the rest.
  • Disclosure packages are not casual reading. They tell you more about the home than any open house ever will.
  • Patience and discipline win in a hot market — not aggression. Buyers who chase end up overpaying for the wrong house.
  • Your agent works for you. A good buyer's agent will tell you when not to write an offer just as readily as they'll tell you when to write one.

What to ask your agent before signing on

  1. How many transactions did you close in Marin in the last 12 months?
  2. What neighborhoods do you know best, and where are you less strong?
  3. Who is on your team? Who actually shows up to inspections?
  4. How do you handle multi-offer situations on my behalf?
  5. What's your communication style — text, email, weekly check-ins?
